# Visualization Guide

This document describes each plot generated by `run_structured.py`,  
how to read it, and what patterns to look for when evaluating Nomadic Intelligence behavior.

All figures referenced here correspond to actual output files in the `save_dir` directory.

---

## Primary Diagnostic Figures

### 1. `fixed_vs_standardmoe_vs_nomadic_test_mse.png`

**What it shows:** Training curves for all four model configurations over 220 epochs.

**Lines:**
| Line | Color | Meaning |
|------|-------|---------|
| Fixed (static) | Gray solid | Single MLP — static baseline |
| Standard MoE (seq) | Teal dashed | MoE without temporal signals |
| Nomadic NoPolicy (seq) | Orange dashed | + Δx + dynamic τₖ |
| Nomadic Full (static) | Purple dotted | Full model, static eval (intentionally poor) |
| Nomadic Full (seq) | Purple solid | Full model, sequential eval (primary metric) |

**What healthy behavior looks like:**
- Nomadic Full (seq, solid purple) should converge below all other seq lines by ~epoch 50–100
- Nomadic Full (static, dotted purple) will be *higher* than Fixed — this is expected and correct
- Standard MoE (seq) should track close to Fixed

**Red flags:**
- Nomadic Full (seq) not separating from Standard MoE → temporal signals not learning
- Nomadic Full (seq) oscillating without convergence → PolicyNet training instability

---

### 2. `stable_vs_transition_entropy.png`

**What it shows:** Gate entropy averaged over stable-phase vs. transition-phase timesteps, plotted over training epochs.

**Lines:**
| Line | Color | Meaning |
|------|-------|---------|
| Stable Entropy | Blue | H(g_t) during stable regime blocks |
| Transition Entropy | Orange | H(g_t) during transition steps |

**What healthy behavior looks like:**
- Stable Entropy (blue) should *decrease* over training, ideally reaching < 0.2
- Transition Entropy (orange) should *remain high* (≥ 0.85), or at least higher than Stable
- The gap between the two lines is ΔH — this should grow over training

**This is the most important single diagnostic figure.**  
A model can achieve low Seq MSE through degenerate means (e.g., always routing to the best-average expert).  
The entropy differentiation pattern confirms that the model is actually *responding contextually* to regime transitions, not just achieving a good static assignment.

**Interpreting the Stable Entropy trajectory seen in experiments:**  
After ~epoch 25–50, Stable Entropy typically drops sharply (blue line) — this coincides with PolicyNet beginning to consistently predict hard routing during stable phases. The occasional spikes after epoch 150 indicate brief instability as the routing landscape shifts, but the overall downward trend confirms convergence.

### 3. `expert_trajectory.png`

**What it shows:** The dominant expert (Top-1 argmax of gate) for each batch in the test sequence.

**X-axis:** Batch index (time-ordered)  
**Y-axis:** Expert ID (0, 1, or 2, for K=3)

**What healthy behavior looks like:**
- Alternating blocks at stable values (e.g., long stretches at Expert 2, then switching to Expert 0)
- The block lengths should correspond roughly to the stable phase duration in the test sequence
- Transitions between blocks should be relatively sharp, not gradual

**What to look for:**
- **Structured switching**: clear block structure with distinct experts per regime → good
- **Expert collapse**: trajectory stays at one value the entire time → DwellTimeRegularizer or load balancing not working
- **Chaotic switching**: rapid flickering at every batch → τₖ constraint not effective

**Note on permutation:** The specific expert ID assigned to each regime varies by seed.  
Seed 42 may assign Regime C → Expert 2; seed 123 may assign Regime C → Expert 1.  
This is normal. What matters is within-run consistency (the same expert per regime throughout a run).

---

### 4. `dwell_time_histogram.png`

**What it shows:** Distribution of consecutive dwell durations on the dominant expert.

**X-axis:** Dwell time (number of consecutive batches on one expert)  
**Y-axis:** Count

**What healthy behavior looks like:**
- Heavy mass at short dwell times (1–3): these are transition steps, where the gate appropriately explores
- A secondary cluster at moderate-to-long dwell times (5–10+): these are stable phase fixations
- The long tail (>8) represents deep fixation in stable phases when τ_dynamic → τ_max

**Interpreting the distribution from experiments:**  
The observed distribution (heavy mass at 1–2, moderate tail at 3–5, small counts at 9–11) is consistent with:
- Transition steps generating many brief 1-step dwells
- Stable phases generating the longer tail
- The gap at 6–8 reflects the τ_dynamic range (2.0–8.0)

**Red flags:**
- Distribution entirely at 1 → pure noise switching, τₖ not working
- Single spike at a large value (e.g., 50+) → fixation collapse, dwell regularizer not releasing

### 5. `policy_hybrid_signals.png`

**What it shows:** PolicyNet behavioral signals over training epochs (dual y-axis).

**Lines:**
| Line | Axis | Meaning |
|------|------|---------|
| Policy Switch Rate | Left (0–1) | Fraction of steps where PolicyNet predicts switch(1) |
| Policy Hard Rate | Left (0–1) | Fraction of steps where PolicyNet predicts hard routing |
| Mean Dynamic Tau | Right | Average τ_dynamic value over test sequence |

**What healthy behavior looks like:**
- **Switch Rate** (~0.95–1.0, high): PolicyNet correctly identifies that most steps — including transition steps — warrant switching pressure. This is not a problem; the Dwell regularizer handles actual commitment.
- **Hard Rate** (gradual increase from ~0.01 → ~0.08–0.10): PolicyNet learns over time to identify stable phases where hard routing is appropriate. The slow increase reflects the difficulty of learning when fixation is safe.
- **Mean Dynamic Tau** (stable, ~6.5–6.7): In the current synthetic task, environmental volatility (σ²_Δ) is relatively low on average → τ_dynamic hovers near τ_max. High τ is correct behavior.

**Interpreting the gap between Switch Rate and Hard Rate:**  
Most steps are classified as "switch" by PolicyNet, but only a small fraction trigger hard routing.  
This reflects the distinction between *routing pressure* (how uncertain the gate should be) and *routing mode* (whether to commit to a single expert).

## Secondary Figures

### `regime_expert_usage_heatmap.png`

**What it shows:** Heatmap of Top-1 expert usage fraction by regime (rows = regimes, columns = experts).

**Reading it:** Bright cells indicate that a regime primarily routes to that expert.  
A good specialization pattern shows one dominant cell per row, with different dominant columns per row.

### `regime_mean_gate_distance.png`

**What it shows:** Pairwise L2 distance between per-regime gate centroids.

**Interpretation:** Higher distance = more distinct routing behavior between regimes.  
This quantifies expert specialization as a continuous measure rather than Top-1 assignment.

### `dynamic_tau_trace.png`

**What it shows:** τ_dynamic(t) over the training sequence, correlated with Δx.

**What to look for:** τ should decrease during regime transitions (high σ²_Δ → lower τ → more switching tolerance) and increase during stable phases. In the synthetic task, the oscillation is subtle because σ²_Δ peaks are brief.

### `switch_latency_curve.png`

**What it shows:** Mean switch latency over training epochs.

**Target:** Moderate and stable. Very low (< 0.5) → gate may be switching before the regime change is fully registered. Very high (> 3.0) → gate responds too slowly.

## Diagnostic Checklist

When evaluating a new run, check figures in this order:

1. **`fixed_vs_standardmoe_vs_nomadic_test_mse.png`** — Is Nomadic Full (seq) converging below Standard MoE (seq)?
2. **`stable_vs_transition_entropy.png`** — Is Stable Entropy decreasing while Transition Entropy stays high?
3. **`expert_trajectory.png`** — Is there a clear block structure with distinct experts per regime?
4. **`policy_hybrid_signals.png`** — Is Hard Rate gradually increasing? Is Mean Tau stable?
5. **`dwell_time_histogram.png`** — Is there a tail at moderate-to-long dwell times?

If all five pass, the model is exhibiting structured adaptive fixation — the core behavioral claim of Nomadic Intelligence.

---

## Key Insight

> These visualizations together demonstrate a single claim:
>
> **Nomadic Intelligence is not random switching.**
> It is a system that has learned, without explicit supervision,
> to *fixate* when the environment is stable and *explore* when it is changing —
> and to do so in a way that is consistent, measurable, and improvable.
>
> The Stable vs. Transition Entropy figure is the clearest single evidence for this claim.
